The purpose of this post is twofold: 

1. to define some mathematical objects and spaces(also for future musical analysis) representing a small subset of what I call the "relevant(to the Western idiom) abstract features" of music, and
3. to discuss and (largely) speculate about the process of the musical perception of these features and perceptual tasks/strategies, and argue against what I call "hyperattention" and suggest how this can be avoided.


## Frequency and Pitch

Represent of the space of all frequencies by the interval $\msc{F} = (0, \infty)$ (conventionally using Hertz as the unit of choice). _Pitch_ is a perceptual property of certain sounds, that allows us to correspond the sound $\rho$ with a single scalar value, denoted by $\operatorname{pitch}(\rho)$. In this way, we may describe a sound $\rho_1$ as "lower" than $\rho_2$ if $\operatorname{pitch}(\rho_1) \leq \operatorname{pitch}(\rho_2)$. Let us represent the space of all pitches by $\msc{P}$. 

For sounds with a "identifiable" fundamental frequency, the perceived pitch is an increasing function of the frequency, although there are <a href="https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/037859557990025X" target="blank">psychoacoustic phenomena</a> in where the pitch may be perceived to very slightly increase or decrease with the amplitude for very high or low frequencies. For more complex sounds one may perceive multiple pitches. The process by which the perception of pitch itself is determined from auditory stimuli, and its nuances, are not the focus of this post. We write $\msc P = \msc F$ as an idealization.

## Pitch <span style='color: red'>C</span><span style='color: orange'>h</span><span style='color: gold'>r</span><span style='color: green'>o</span><span style='color: blue'>m</span><span style='color: purple'>a</span>

Consider the equivalence relation $\sim_{\rm C}$ on $\msc{P}$ generated by the equivalences $\nu \sim_{\rm C} 2\nu$ (after identifying with $\msc{F}$). Taking the quotient yields the space of _pitch chromas_ or _pitch classes_ $\msc{P}\_{\rm C} = \msc{P} \mathbin{/} {\sim_{\rm C}}$. Choosing an arbitrary  "middle" pitch/frequency $\nu_0$, one obtains a bijection
\begin{equation}
	\msc{P}\_{\rm C} \times \mathbb{Z} \to \msc{P}; \quad ([\nu], j) \mapsto [\nu] \cap [2^{j}\nu_0, 2^{j+1}\nu_0],
\end{equation}
where $[\nu]$ is the equivalence class of $\nu$ under $\sim_{\rm C}$. This reveals a secondary aspect of alongside chroma, the _height_, written here as $j$(which admittedly depends on the choice of $\nu_0$).

## Relative <span style='color: red'>C</span><span style='color: orange'>h</span><span style='color: gold'>r</span><span style='color: green'>o</span><span style='color: blue'>m</span><span style='color: purple'>a</span>

The perception of chroma can be further "colored" by an internal sense of a pitch chroma called a _tonic_. In a future post, I plan to discuss and speculate on the processes by which we perceive a tonic from listening to music; for now, we can think of a tonic as a pitch chroma which

1. embeds itself within the mind in such a way that it may not directly related to any particular stimulus, and
2. allows for the processing of features which I call _relative pitch chroma_ from pitches.

We can represent relative chroma as follows. Consider the product $\mathscr{P}\_{\rm C} \times \mathscr{P}\_{\rm C}$ representing a perceived pitch chroma and underlying tonic. Now one can quotient by the equivalence relation $\sim_{\rm RC}$ where $([\nu_1], [\nu_2]) \sim_{\rm RC} ([\lambda\nu_1], [\lambda\nu_2])$ for any constant $\lambda > 0$ to yield the space of relative chroma $\mathscr{P}\_{\rm RC} = (\mathscr{P}\_{\rm C} \times \mathscr{P}\_{\rm C}) \mathbin{/} {\sim_{\rm RC}}$. We denote the equivalence class of $([\nu_1], [\nu_2])$ by $[\nu_1] : [\nu_2]$. In the future when necessary, I will distinguish between pitch chroma and relative chroma by using the term _absolute_ chroma for the former.

The objects I describe above are better thought as lying in a kind of space rather than a set with no additional structure. For example, if we give $\mathscr{P}$ the order topology and consider all our constructions as topological ones, then $\mathscr{P}\_{\rm C}$ and $\mathscr{P}\_{\rm RC}$ are homeomorphic to the circle $\mb S^1 = \R / \Z$. Specifically, we have maps(the first considers a unitless value of the frequency in Hertz)
\begin{aligned}
	\mathscr{P}\_{\rm C} \to \mb{S}^1&; \quad [\nu] \mapsto [\log_2(\nu)], \\\	\mathscr{P}\_{\rm RC} \to \mb{S}^1&; \quad ([\nu_1] : [\nu_2]) \mapsto [\log_2(\nu_1 / \nu_2)].
\end{aligned}

A listener of Western music with AP or RP might possess <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Categorical_perception" target="blank">categories</a> for absolute or relative chroma represented by the following labeled regions on the circle.

## Harmony

### Harmonic Shape

Consider the collection of multisets of size $k$ of $\msc{P}$, denoted by $S^k(\msc{P}) = \\{\\{p_1, \ldots, p_k\\} \mid p_i \in \msc{P}\\}$. Then consider the equivalence relation $\sim_{\rm H}$ on $S^k(\msc{P})$ generated by $A \sim_{\rm H} \lambda A$ for any constant $\lambda > 0$. Here, $\lambda A = \\{ \lambda \nu \mid \nu \in A\\}$ is a _scaling_ of $B$. The quotient $S^k(\msc{P}) \mathbin{/} \{\sim_{\rm H}\}$ is called the space of _harmonic shapes_ of size $k$.

## Perception

_Perception_ is a process in which the brain automatically synthesizes and inteprets high-level constructs or representations from raw sensory input. I will distinguish between _bottom-up_, "implicit" perception, which is the subconscious processing of low- to high-level information, and _top-down_, "explicit" perception which is the process of accessing these high-level constructs. 

I define a _perceptual task_ to be a task in which one is presented stimuli and must discriminate or identify the stimuli based on their abstract features. I define _perceptual strategy_ to be the mechanism by which they use their perceptions to complete the task. A well-known phenomenom called _perceptual learning_ demonstrates that performing perceptual tasks can actually improve one's perception.

I divide perceptual strategies into being either direct or indirect. A direct perceptual strategy is one in which one's response is directly and immediately determined by a percept accessed from top-down perception. A direct strategy is therefore mostly unconscious and is limited by the efficiency and fidelity of the perceptual process. In an indirect perceptual strategy, one relies on strategies beyond the act of immediate perception to determine their response. Such as strategy may involve conscious decisions and is further limited by the efficiency and validity of the decision process.

### Implict and Explicit Perception

I think this account of perception is in line with a commonly accepted theory in cognitive neuroscience called <a href="https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1364661304002153" target="blank">reverse hierarchy theory</a>(RHT). It posits that perception consists of bottom-up processing and our conscious, explicit, perception starts at the highest levels of this hierarchy and moves downwards.

	We see and hear objects rather than their local constituents. </blockquote>

### Perceptual Strategies

I believe it is musically important for one to be able to succeed in many of the perceptual tasks corresponding to the abstract features I defined earlier. One such perceptual task is _categorization_, in which one must identify each stimulus as belonging to some determined category. It is thought that the ability to categorize absolute chroma, referred to as absolute pitch(AP), involves two subprocesses:

- a perceptual process which forms discrete categories from the continuum of absolute chroma, and 
- an associative memory process to provide labels to these categories.
 
 The alternative view is that the perceptual process only involves processing absolute chroma, and labels are associated to absolute chroma itself without the additional perceptual categorization process. Either way, there is some associative process which is needed for a categorization task. The prevalence of AP in the general and musical population is exceedingly low. My pet hypothesis on why this is that after some point in early life, constructs based on the relative nature of music are more strongly associated to labels, and this inhibits an absolute labeling(e.g. a child associates the label "Three Blind Mice" to "$\text{mi, re, do}$," so there is no "need" to associate it to the starting note $\text{F}\sharp$). In this view, the puzzling question is not why so few possess absolute pitch(my proposed answer would be that it requires some type of musical intervention before the brain starts to preferentially prefer the relative mindset), instead, I wonder why humans seem to have adopted a relative understanding of music.

 Whether or not this particular details of this hypothesis are true, I argue that the theme behind this logic is in fact applicable to music perception as a whole, and suggests how one should learn to perceive the features I mention. Specifically, I propose that:

- In a given perceptual task, "hyperattention" to lower-level information on the perceptual hierarchy, or to information unrelated to the perceptual task, may inhibit the process of top-down perceptual learning as posited by RHT, if this information can be used to form an indirect strategy. Consequently, with poor learning, high-level representations of abstract features have lower fidelity and so the use direct strategies(relying on only immediate top-down access) in perceptual tasks is also inhibited.

By "hyperattention," I refer to preferential access and conscious attention given to information in a way differing from the usual, top-down access posited by RHT. See the following excerpts from abstracts from <a href="https://doi.org/10.2307/40285562" target="blank">two</a> <a href="https://doi.org/10.2307/40285599" target="blank">papers</a> by Ken'ichi Miyazaki:

<blockquote cite="https://doi.org/10.2307/40285562">
	In this study, AP possessors tried to identify the musical intervals between pairs of successive tones. The first tone (a reference) was either in-tune C according to the conventional pitch standard or out-of-tune C (a quarter-tone higher than standard C). The identification performance was less accurate and slower in the out-of-tune reference condition than in the in-tune condition. In contrast, AP nonpossessors showed no significant difference in performance in the two conditions, as predicted by the principle of equality under transposition.
</blockquote>

<blockquote cite="https://doi.org/10.2307/40285599">
	Listeners tried to identify melodic intervals of various sizes (260–540 cents) presented in three different tonal contexts established by a preceding tonal cadence. Listeners without absolute pitch showed equal accuracy and speed in doing the task in the three tonal contexts, as expected from the principle of transposability of musical pitch relations. In contrast, some absolute pitch listeners snowed a marked decline in accuracy and longer response times in the F# major and the out-of-tune E major contexts compared with the C major context. This result suggests that some absolute pitch listeners are relatively poor in identifying pitch relations in tonal contexts and sometimes tend to stick to absolute pitch even in a task that needs relative pitch, resulting in poor performance in perceiving musical pitch relations.
</blockquote>

Under my hypothesis, this can be explained as AP possessors defaulting to an _indirect_ strategy of either mental calculation or association(for example associating a pair $(\text{D}, \text{F}\sharp)$ to a major third) to succeed in interval identification instead of a direct strategy relying on an immediate perception of the interval. This would be due to the preferential attention given to the absolute chroma categories commonly used in the Western idiom. Thus perceptual learning is inhibited and AP possessors often fail to form accurate neural representations of the high-level abstract feature. When presented with an interval consisting of notes which don't neatly fall into these absolute chroma categories, the indirect strategy is rendered invalid.

Another (anecdotal) example is what I find myself doing sometimes when presented the task of identifying intervals. If I have a tonal center, I often rely on the relative chroma category of each note to determine the appropriate interval. Subjectively, I find that I am not _hearing_ the interval, but rather _deducing it_. As a result, my immediate perception of harmonic intervals is currently more weak than I would like it to be.

This hypothesis argues against a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_recognition#Reference_songs" target="blank">commonly taught technique</a> of interval recognition. The linked page suggests a myriad of songs that will supposedly teach one to perceive a given interval commonly used in the Western idiom. However, these songs are heard and thought of in a tonal context. Thus, when one says "use the second and third notes of 'Ode to Joy' to learn an ascending minor second," what is likely to happen is that one learns that "$\text{fa}$ above $\text{mi}$" _constitutes_ a minor second. When asked to identify a minor second, such a person will more likely than not mentally recall "Ode to Joy," refitting the heard minor second in the context of the piece. Thus even if in context, the minor second is $\text{ti-do}$, such a person must refit it mentally to sound as $\text{mi-fa}$ in order to recognize it. I believe this is really an indirect strategy, and will cause inefficiencies and inaccuracies when in unclear tonal contexts or contexts that do not match the "reference songs" learned.


### Ear Training

While I have presented an argument that functionally, in terms of completing perceptual tasks, one is benefited by a direct strategy and inhibited by hyperattention, I would also argue that subjectively, one's musical experience is simply broadened by the ability to immediately perceive both high-level and low-level abstract features. One needs to see the forest for the trees, and yet recognize how each individual tree comprises the forest. So to clarify, I don't mean to argue that indirect strategies are never useful, but rather that more direct strategies represent an immediate perception more in line with the feature at hand, and that hyperattention makes this more difficult.

This gives some suggestions on how one should musically train their ear. In particular one should focus on perceiving "coarser" features before moving on to "finer" features. This precludes the possibility of hyperattention to a "fine" feature and the use of an indirect strategy to deduce coarser features. I suggest a guideline for determining what features are coarse and fine based on the spaces each feature lies in and the possibility of "canonical" maps existing between them. The existence of such a map(say from $X$ to $Y$) implies the existence of an indirect strategy that determines a feature in $Y$ from one in $X$. However, indirect strategies do have their use and are sometimes unavoidable. I would suggest an indirect strategy only when it leaves no possibility of inaccurate or inefficient performance, in contrast to the absolute pitch strategies we saw earlier.

In practice, this means if one were to "start from scratch" I would suggest one to:

1. Learn how to hear elements of $S^2(\mathscr{P}\_{\rm C})/{\sim_{\rm H}}$, "harmonic content" of size 2. In practice, this involves recognizing and categorizing into 6 categories.

2. Learn how to hear harmonic intervals($S^2(\mathscr{P})/{\sim_{\rm H}}$) and relative chroma($\mathscr{P}\_{\rm SC}$, 12 categories) at roughly the same pace. This order stems from the fact that they both have a reasonably "canonical" map to $S^2(\mathscr{P_{\rm C}})/{\sim_{\rm H}}$. Additionally, I suspect learning 1. will make this step significantly easier.

3. Learn how to hear larger harmonic shapes and contents. Here an indirect strategy of perceiving smaller "subshapes" or "subcontents" and determining how they "fit" together may be useful.
